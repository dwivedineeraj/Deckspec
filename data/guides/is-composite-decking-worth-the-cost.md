# Is Composite Decking Worth the Cost? ROI vs Pressure-Treated Wood (2026)

Every homeowner building a deck faces the same question: pay more upfront for composite or save now with pressure-treated wood? The answer depends on how long you plan to stay in your home, your tolerance for maintenance, and how you value your time.

Here's the data-driven breakdown.

## Upfront Cost Comparison

Let's start with material-only prices for a typical 300 sq ft deck:

| Material | Material Cost/sq ft | Total Materials | Installed Cost (300 sq ft) |
|----------|-------------------|----------------|---------------------------|
| Pressure-treated pine | $3–$6 | $900–$1,800 | $7,500–$12,900 |
| Entry composite (Trex Enhance, Fiberon Good Life) | $3–$5 | $900–$1,500 | $9,000–$13,500 |
| Mid-range composite (Trex Select, TimberTech Edge+) | $4–$7 | $1,200–$2,100 | $10,500–$16,500 |
| Premium composite (Trex Transcend, TimberTech Legacy) | $6–$11 | $1,800–$3,300 | $13,500–$22,500 |
| Premium PVC (Azek Vintage, Fiberon Paramount) | $8–$14 | $2,400–$4,200 | $15,000–$25,500 |

The upfront premium for composite over pressure-treated is typically **$1,500–$5,100** on a 300 sq ft deck.

## 20-Year Total Cost of Ownership

Here's where the math flips. Pressure-treated wood requires ongoing maintenance that composite doesn't.

| Cost Component | Pressure-Treated | Entry Composite | Premium Composite |
|---------------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Initial install (300 sq ft) | $10,000 | $11,000 | $18,000 |
| Staining/sealing (every 2–3 years × 20 yrs) | $3,000–$4,500 | $0 | $0 |
| Board replacement (warping, rot) | $600–$1,200 | $0–$300 | $0 |
| Full replacement at year 15 | $10,000 | $0 | $0 |
| **20-year total** | **$23,600–$25,700** | **$11,000–$11,300** | **$18,000** |

Based on industry data from contractor pricing guides, a pressure-treated deck costs roughly **$23,000–$26,000 over 20 years** when you account for the annual staining/sealing ($300–$450 every 2–3 years DIY, or $600–$1,200 professionally) and full replacement at year 12–15.

An entry-level composite deck like **[Trex Enhance](/brands/trex/enhance)** or **[Fiberon Good Life](/brands/fiberon/good-life)** costs **$11,000–$13,500 over 20 years** — roughly **half the total cost** of wood, even though the upfront price was similar or slightly higher.

Premium composite like **[Trex Transcend](/brands/trex/transcend)** or **[TimberTech Legacy](/brands/timbertech/legacy)** costs more upfront but still beats wood on 20-year total cost.

## Resale Value

According to Remodeling Magazine's 2025 Cost vs. Value Report:

- **Wood deck addition**: 66.8% ROI (cost: $17,051; resale value: $11,390)
- **Composite deck addition**: 54.2% ROI (cost: $24,206; resale value: $13,131)

Wood recovers a higher percentage, but that's because it starts cheaper. In absolute dollars, a composite deck adds more to your home's value.

Real estate agents in many markets report that composite decks are increasingly expected by buyers in the $400K+ price range. A pressure-treated deck at that level can feel like a compromise, while a composite deck is a selling point.

## Maintenance Time Comparison

This is where composite's value becomes personal:

**Pressure-treated wood:**
- Annual washing and inspection: 2–4 hours
- Re-staining/sealing every 2–3 years: 8–16 hours per session
- Spot repairs (splinters, warped boards): 1–2 hours per season
- Total: **15–25 hours per year**

**Composite or PVC decking:**
- Sweeping: 15 minutes per week
- Soap-and-water wash: 1–2 hours twice a year
- Stain spot treatment: 30 minutes as needed
- Total: **2–5 hours per year**

If you value your time at $50/hour, the 20-year maintenance savings alone are worth **$12,000–$20,000** for composite.

## Climate Considerations

Some situations strongly favor composite:

- **High humidity**: Wood warps, cracks, and rots faster. Composites with good moisture resistance (like [MoistureShield Vision](/brands/moistureshield/vision)) hold up far better.
- **Wildfire zones**: PVC decking like [Azek](/brands/timbertech/vintage) and [Trex Refuge](/brands/trex/refuge) offer Class A fire ratings essential in WUI (Wildland-Urban Interface) areas.
- **Poolside**: PVC's waterproof nature makes it ideal around pools. Wood can warp from constant moisture exposure.
- **Coastal**: Salt air accelerates wood decay. Composites resist salt damage much better.

And some situations still favor wood:

- **Short ownership**: If you plan to sell the house in 3–5 years, pressure-treated is the cheaper option that still delivers decent ROI.
- **Rental properties**: Lower upfront cost makes sense when you won't be around to enjoy the deck.
- **Strict budget**: If the choice is a small pressure-treated deck vs no deck at all, build the wood deck.

## Environmental Impact

Composite decking has a compelling environmental story. [Trex](/brands/trex) boards contain 95% recycled materials — reclaimed sawdust and recycled plastic film (the equivalent of thousands of plastic bags per deck). [Fiberon](/brands/fiberon) uses 94% recycled content. Pressure-treated wood uses virgin lumber treated with copper-based preservatives that leach into soil over time.

PVC decking ([Azek](/brands/timbertech/vintage), [Fiberon Paramount](/brands/fiberon/paramount)) has a higher carbon footprint since it's 100% virgin polymer. However, its 50-year lifespan means fewer replacements over the long term.

## The Verdict

**Composite decking is worth the cost if:**
- You plan to stay in your home 7+ years
- You value your weekends over maintenance
- Your budget can handle the $1,500–$5,000 upfront premium
- You're building in a humid, coastal, or fire-prone area

**Pressure-treated wood still makes sense if:**
- You're on a tight budget and a deck is more important than the material
- You plan to move within 5 years
- You genuinely enjoy woodworking and maintenance
- The deck is for a rental or secondary property

For most homeowners building in 2026, the 20-year cost math favors composite. The break-even point is typically **year 7–10**, when cumulative wood maintenance costs surpass the initial composite premium. After that, composite is pure savings.
